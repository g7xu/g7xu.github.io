I got into one of the best master's program in the world. I got the internship offer from large corp I had been chasing. These things, I lost sleep for months wanting for. And then they happened. The happiness lasted a few days. Then, I started comparing myself to every other admitted student. Every peer at a bigger
company. I became scared of lossing again! Lossing used to be the thing I embraced, but I just drifted into it again! I write to this remind myself again to be the captain I want to be.

## Winning or Losing gives you direction, not a final destination

A captain treats results as feedback. The higher you let success lift you, the
harder failure will pull you down. A great captain keeps his feet on the deck in
either case — because he knows that in adversity, mindset is the channel that
leads him to his destination.

Success is a lighthouse. It tells you something you did was right. It points you
in the right direction. Failure is a rough storm. It tells you something is broken.
Find it. Fix it. Move on.

The best captains aren't the ones who never hit storms. They're the ones who think
clearest when the waves are highest.

Celebrate, but briefly. Grieve, but briefly. Then steer.

## Listen and think

Calmly take in everything around you: arguments from your crew, actions made by other ships, and the storm you are facing. Then figure out which of this actually gets you closer to your goal?

Dare to be a student of the weak. Dare to be a critic of the strong. Dare to engage with intensive discussions, and dare to make hard decisions in the
long stretches of loneliness.

## Think with a clear head

When everyone around you is shouting, the captain has to be the calmest person
on deck.

Listen to your crew. Watch the other ships. Read the weather. Then ask: which of
this actually helps me get where I want to go?

Dare to be a beginner. Ask why. Ask people to teach you. Sit in the discomfort
of not knowing. The best thinking happens in solitude — away from the noise of
what everyone else is doing.

The wheel is in your hands. Not the crowd's.

---

## Know your people and your world

A captain doesn't have to be the best sailor on the ship. Not the most
experienced. Not the most technical.

But he has to know people.

He knows what his crew needs. What they're best at. Who to put at the helm in a
storm, and who to trust when the waters go quiet. He listens — not just to his
crew, but to the world. New winds. New information. He takes it all in and adjusts
his course. Not because he doubts his destination. But because the best path there
is always changing.

---

## Be obsessed

A captain who isn't sure he'll arrive makes his crew unsure too.

Be unreasonable about your destination. You don't need to know when. You don't
need to know how rough the next storm will be. But you cannot doubt where you're
going.

That obsession is what carries everyone through the dark stretches. The long
nights with no wind, no stars, no signal. They keep going because you keep going.

Every time the ship gets knocked down, get back up. Not because it's easy.
Because you can't imagine stopping.

That's the captain I want to become.
