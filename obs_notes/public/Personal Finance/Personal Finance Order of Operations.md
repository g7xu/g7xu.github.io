**Summary**: The r/personalfinance "Prime Directive" — an ordered flowchart for what to do with each dollar of income, from budgeting through retirement and other goals. Do the steps in order; don't skip steps that apply to you.

This is the canonical answer to the most common r/personalfinance question: *"I have $X, what should I do with it?"* The core idea is **sequence**: money flows down a priority list, and you only move to the next step once the current one is satisfied. The ordering is designed to capture the highest **guaranteed, risk-free returns first** (employer match, paying off high-interest debt) before reaching for uncertain market returns.

![[personal_income_spending_flowchart.jpg]]

## The ordered steps

**Step 0 — [[Budgeting|Budget and reduce expenses]], set realistic goals.** Know where your money goes. Minimize expenses where practical (entertainment, eating out, and clothing are easier to cut than housing or food). Then define goals: retirement, a house, a car.

**Step 1 — Build a starter [[Emergency Fund]].** A liquid sum you don't touch unless something unexpected happens. Full size is 3–6 months of expenses, but while you still have high-interest debt, a smaller $1,000 (or 1-month) buffer is acceptable.

**Step 2 — Capture the [[401(k) Employer Match|employer match]].** Contribute enough to your employer-sponsored plan to get the full match. This comes *before* paying off high-interest debt because the match is a risk-free, instant return (e.g. an instant 50% ROI) usually higher than any debt rate.

**Step 3 — Pay down [[High-Interest Debt Payoff|high-interest debt]]** (roughly >4%). Make minimum payments on everything first, then attack the rest using the **avalanche** (highest rate first) or **snowball** (smallest balance first) method.

**Step 4 — Contribute to an [[IRA & Roth vs Traditional|IRA]].** Aim for ~15% of gross income up to the annual limit ($7,000). IRAs usually offer better, lower-cost fund choices than employer plans — so they come before *rounding out* the 401(k).

**Step 5 — Save more for retirement.** Go back and increase 401(k)/403(b) contributions (or open a solo plan if self-employed) toward 15–20% of gross income total. Always use tax-advantaged space before a taxable account, *even if your 401(k) is mediocre.*

**Step 6 — [[Saving for Other Goals|Save for other goals]].** Now there's flexibility: [[HSA & 529 Accounts|HSA]] for medical, [[HSA & 529 Accounts|529]] for education, down payments, vehicles, early retirement, or giving. Match the account type to the time horizon.

**Advanced methods.** Over the Roth income limit → [[IRA & Roth vs Traditional#Backdoor Roth|backdoor Roth]]. After-tax 401(k) space → mega backdoor Roth. An HSA can double as a stealth retirement account.

## Why this order

| Earlier steps           | Why they win                                          |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Emergency fund          | Prevents new high-interest debt when life happens     |
| Employer match          | Guaranteed ~50–100% return, beats everything          |
| High-interest debt      | Guaranteed return = the interest rate you stop paying |
| Tax-advantaged accounts | Tax savings compound for decades                      |


The recurring principle: **a guaranteed return beats a probable one.** Paying a 10% loan is a certain 10%; the stock market's ~historical return is only an average, not a promise. That's why debt above ~4% is paid early and lower-rate debt can be stretched.
