**Summary**: Step 3 of the [[Personal Finance Order of Operations]] — after capturing the employer match, attack debts above ~4% using the avalanche or snowball method.

**Sources**: `Reddit Basic Finance.md`

**Last updated**: 2026-06-20

---

After securing the [[401(k) Employer Match]], direct extra money at **high-interest debt** (much over 4%). Always make the **minimum payments on every debt first** (so they're recorded on time), then throw extra money at one target debt.

## Two methods

- **Avalanche** — pay debts in order of **interest rate**, highest first. Financially optimal: you pay the least total interest.
- **Snowball** — (popularized by Dave Ramsey) pay in order of **balance size**, smallest first. Gives a psychological boost and frees up minimum payments faster, but leaves larger/higher-rate loans accruing longer, costing more overall.

r/personalfinance favors **avalanche**, but the psychological pull of snowball is real — the most important thing is to *start and keep going*. You can switch methods later.

### Worked example

Debtor Dan:
- Loan A: $1,100, $100/mo min, **5%**
- Loan B: $3,300, $300/mo min, **10%**
- Windfall: $2,000

First pay $100 + $300 = **$400** in minimums. The remaining **$1,600** goes to:
- **Snowball** → Loan A (smallest), wiping it out with $600 left over for Loan B.
- **Avalanche** → Loan B (highest rate) entirely.

(Tools like unbury.me estimate how long each method takes and total interest.)

## What counts as "low enough" to just pay minimums?

The rough consensus: **pay off anything above ~4% early**; debt under ~4% can be stretched and paid at the minimum while you invest instead. The caveat: paying a loan is a **guaranteed** return equal to its rate, whereas beating it in the market is only *probable*.

**Don't stretch a loan to boost your credit score** — never pay extra interest for that. Interest rate should be the *sole* factor in whether you pay a loan down early.
