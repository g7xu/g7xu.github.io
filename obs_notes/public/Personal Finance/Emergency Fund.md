**Summary**: Step 1 of the [[Personal Finance Order of Operations]] — a liquid reserve you only touch for genuine emergencies, sized 3–6 months of expenses (or a $1,000 starter while paying off high-interest debt).

**Sources**: `Reddit Basic Finance.md`

**Last updated**: 2026-06-20

---

An emergency fund is a **relatively liquid sum you don't touch** unless something unexpected comes up — unexpected travel, an essential appliance replacement, a sudden medical procedure. If you ever draw from it, your first priority once back on your feet is to **replenish it**.

## How to size it

- **Most people**: 3 to 6 months of expenses.
- **Variable / uncertain income**: a larger fund of 9 to 12 months.
- **While paying off high-interest debt**: a smaller starter fund of **$1,000 (or 1 month of expenses)** is temporarily acceptable. This pairs with the order-of-operations logic — see [[High-Interest Debt Payoff]]. The threshold for "high interest" here is debts above ~10%.

## Where to hold it

The fund must be **safe and quickly liquidatable**. Good and bad choices:

| Use                                       | Avoid                                     |
| ----------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| FDIC-insured savings/checking             | Stocks (volatile)                         |
| CDs (mind early-withdrawal penalties)     | Credit cards                              |
| I bonds (only after the 12-month lock-up) | HELOCs / lines of credit (can be revoked) |

Note on **I bonds**: redeeming before 5 years costs the last 3 months of interest, and they can't be touched at all in the first 12 months — so they only suit emergency savings you've already held a while.

The rule: never hold an emergency fund in anything **volatile in value or that can be taken away without warning.**
