**Sources**: `Reddit Basic Finance.md`

**Last updated**: 2026-06-20

## Step 4 — Contribute to an IRA

After high-interest debt is handled, contribute to an **IRA** for the current tax year (you can also contribute for the previous year between Jan 1 and Apr 15). Target ~**15% of gross income** up to the annual limit of **$7,000**.

**Why an IRA before maxing the 401(k)?** You open an IRA with the provider of your choice, so it usually has **better, lower-cost fund options** than an employer plan — low-cost providers like Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab offer cheap index funds.

**When to swap Steps 4 and 5** (max the employer plan first):
- Your employer plan already has excellent low-cost index funds (domestic stock <0.1%, international <0.2%, bond <0.1% expense ratios), **or**
- You have access to the federal **Thrift Savings Plan (TSP)**, **or**
- You exceed the IRA income limit for a deductible Traditional contribution *and* a backdoor Roth isn't practical for you.

### Roth vs Traditional

- **Traditional** — contribute pre-tax now, pay tax on withdrawals in retirement.
- **Roth** — contribute after-tax now, withdrawals are tax-free later.

The source defers to r/personalfinance's dedicated "Roth or Traditional" wiki for the full decision; the broad heuristic is Roth when your tax rate is lower now than you expect in retirement.

### Backdoor Roth

If you're **over the Roth income limits**, contribute to a Traditional IRA and convert it — the "backdoor Roth" — to still complete Step 4. Watch out for pro-rata taxes if you hold other pre-tax IRA balances.

## Step 5 — Save more for retirement

Once the IRA is funded, go back and **round out employer-plan contributions** as much as your budget allows, targeting **15–20% of gross income** total for retirement.

- Contributions again must come from **payroll deductions** — adjust your paycheck %.
- **Self-employed** → open an Individual (solo) 401(k), SEP-IRA, or SIMPLE IRA.
- **No employer plan** → use a taxable account (and ask your employer to add a plan).
- **Behind on savings** → save more than 15% if you can; if you can't hit 15%, start at 10% or whatever you can and increase over time.

**"My 401(k) is awful — still contribute?"** Yes. Always use tax-advantaged space before a taxable account. High fees only bite over very long horizons, and plans are portable — after leaving a job you can roll a bad 401(k) into a great IRA.

### Advanced: mega backdoor Roth

If your 401(k)/403(b) allows **after-tax contributions** (distinct from Roth contributions), you can do a **mega backdoor Roth** to move large sums into Roth space.
